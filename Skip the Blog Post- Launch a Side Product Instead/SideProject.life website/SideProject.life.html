Examples of side projects built by others.

  • Unsplash.com Built by Crew.co.

Crew builds websites. They had a hard time finding quality stock images online for building websites. So, they created this resource.

Unsplash is now it’s own company. Yeah, it’s a very popular site now.

  • How much to make Built by Crew.co. Crew builds websites, designs mobile apps, and designs logos. They built this resource to drive leads to the Crew.co website and to also give expectations to future customers of the costs associated with building these projects.

  • Moodboard Built by Crew.co.

People can come in, record all their ideas for how they want their website to look, then drive a lead into Crew.co to building that website for them.

  • How to build an online business Built by Crew.co.

Online course for entrepreneurs to learn how to sell online. Drives leads into Crew.co to build websites for these online businesses.

  • The Beginner’s Guide to Faster Websites Built by Git Tower.

Git Tower is a tool for software/web developers. What is a problem that these people have? They build websites, but they may not know how to make them fast. Git Tower created an eBook telling you how to create fast websites and leading these devs to the Git Tower software.

There are tons more of these example projects around. Please, share your ideas with me and I will add them to this list. Contact me on the bottom of my site or message me on Twitter and I will add it to the list.

Resources for building side projects.

After you have an idea for what to build, it’s time to actually build it.

We are very lucky because today it has never been easier to build side projects. It is highly recommended to leverage existing products out there to help build your side project. You will get it out sooner and will be able to maintain it much easier.

Build a website

  • Tumblr Unsplash.com was built in 3 hours and $19 to handle over 1 million visitors to the site. That is also what this site is built on!
  • SquareSpace I have personally never used SquareSpace but I do know it’s an option for others to creating a website.

Create a newsletter

  • Mailchimp Great newsletter service. Free to start, easy to use, powerful for all your needs. Send drip campaigns, schedule emails.
  • Revue Never used this myself. It is good for users who are looking to share online articles with users.

Online store for digital or physical products

  • Gumroad Awesome service. Affordable, quick to get going, sell digital or physical goods.

Launch online course

  • Coach This is what I personally use to launch online courses. Awesome service that does everything you need, create newsletters, drip campaigns, hosts all your content.
  • Teachable I have never used this myself. I hear it’s a good resource, however.

Blog

  • Medium Free, quick, easy, big community of readers. Medium is very popular today with publishing blog contenet. I highly recommend checking it out.
  • Ghost My favorite blogging platform. I use Ghost for my personal blog and also post to Medium as well. Open source, collaborate with your team.

Podcast

  • Simplecast This is what I personally use for my podcast. Creates a website for you, hosts all your episodes, helps you to get it on iTunes, Google Play, Stitcher. Great service I highly recommend.

This is not all of the resources out there for you. Please, share your ideas with me and I will add them to this list. Contact me on the bottom of my site or message me on Twitter and I will add it to the list.

How to think of ideas for side projects.

To think of side projects to build, you need to first know your customer. If you have a brand new product that just released and you are not very sure about who your customer is, this is a challenging step for you. You may not be as successful with your side project at this point. It may still be worth a try, but keep in mind you may be attracting the wrong audience.

When thinking of ideas for side projects, You need to be your customer. Get in their head. What problems do they have? From the minute they wake up until they go to bed. What problems do they have in their life that you want to solve?

There are 4 different methods that I use to think of side project ideas.

  1. Do I have knowledge to share?
  2. Can I reuse what is already built?
  3. Can I build an audience?
  4. Do you have problems internally?

Let’s go into these a little more.

Do I have knowledge to share?

I can guarantee that each and every one of you have knowledge in your head that someone else would find useful. What skill have you learned how to do it? What life experiences have you had recently that someone has already dreamed of doing?

With this knowledge, create eBooks, online video courses, webinars, YouTube videos, blog series sharing this knowledge with others.

Jason Fried, the CEO of Basecamp, takes advantage of this. The company Basecamp is a small business that serves hundreds of thousands of paying customers a day. They like to go against the “startup scene” of getting billion dollar investments and trying to create the next unicorn. They were remote before remote was cool. Jason writes every single day about life in the business world. The books they have developed as a company are extremely well done and brings many new ideas to the business world.

Justin Jackson, justinjackson.ca, is a solopreneur. He is very good about sharing knowledge he has. Whenever he learns anything new, he shares it. Creates an eBook, an online video course. In fact, he created his first online course late last year and then after he was done doing that, he created a video course on how to create video courses!

Can I reuse what is already built?

You have spent a ton of effort building your core product at your business. Your blood and sweat has been put into this product. This big product that you have built more then likely has solved many problems inside of it.

Do you think you could break apart your main core product of your business to sell small pieces of it by themselves? Could you take your main code base and open source some parts of it? Could you take your product and reuse it for another purpose?

Is there an opportuntity out there to license your product? This awesome core product you built, does someone else find it useful somehow you can license out to?

Nomadlist.com, a website for digital nomads to find great places around the world to work and live. Levels.io, the founder of Nomadlist, is really good at this. He built Nomadlist from scratch himself. When the Brexit situation was going on, Levels released a new product, Escape the Brexit. When Trump got elected in the United States, he launched, Escape the Trump. 2 new websites that takes existing data from NomadList of awesome places to live and launched 2 new products that got lots of attention and essentially relaunched NomadList a couple times.

Your Circle, a virtual support group mobile app I am the founder of. Your Circle is a mobile app specifically for cancer patients and their families, but I am talking to organizations with adoption and low income high schoolers wanting to go to college to see if they can license the tech to them. These are others who find the technology that is built for cancer patients to be useful for them as well.

Can I build an audience?

As you build this core product of yours, do you have an opportunity to grow a community? Grow an audience?

You can launch a forum online community, a newsletter, a podcast, a new blog all about starting conversations.

Justin Jackson, justinjackson.ca, is a solopreneur. I bring Justin up again because he is also very good at this. He has a community of solopreneurs together to chat about products and business together. He has a newsletter he shares stories about his experiences with. He has a podcast to companion his blog.

January of 2016, Justin decided he was going to make 100 things in 2016. He called it the 2016 Maker Challenge. He could have easily created this in his basement on his own and made some tweets about it every few days about progress. However, Justin created a newsletter inviting others to also build 100 things in 2016. This is actually how I found out about Justin. From others sharing things they are making and referring back to this challenge Justin created.

Jason Fried, the CEO of Basecamp, to mention him again as well. As I said before, Basecamp is loud and proud of their business. They love being different. Jason, through his writing and talking, has built an audience of companies who want to go against the norm of the corporate business we are all used to. Not having meetings. Being remote. Not hiring like crazy to grow. He shares this mindset and gets others excited about his ideas and in turn, they join the Basecamp product.

Do I have problems internally?

Do you have issues as a team working together? Is there a unique way about the way you work you can blog about? What are the problems you as a company are facing you can fix (aka: scratching your own itch).

Crew.co, a marketplace for freelance designers. Crew builds websites. While building websites, they were having a hard time finding good quality stock photography. So, they launched Unsplash.com. Believe me, Crew and the rest of the world doesn’t have a very big problem with finding stock photos anymore.

37 Signals, a design consulting business. 37 Signals started in 1999 in Chicago. As they worked with clients, they had issues together on managing projects. Managing clients. Managing their team. So, they scratched their own itch by building side projects to solve these problems of theirs. Then, after using them internally, they started to sell these products on the side. Then, a few years later, they quit 37 Signals and started Basecamp. Yes. Basecamp originated as a side project in a consulting business.
